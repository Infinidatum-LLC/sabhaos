# Source Quality Heuristics

Pramana ranks sources by tier. Not all sources are equal — use this guide to decide what to cite.

## Tier A — primary documents (cite freely)

- Regulation text, statutes, official guidance documents
- Financial filings (10-K, 10-Q, S-1, court filings)
- Original peer-reviewed studies, working papers from established institutions
- Direct press releases from named parties (with awareness they are partisan)
- Official datasets (Census, BLS, World Bank, central banks, etc.)
- Direct interviews or named-source statements published in reputable outlets

**Why**: closest to ground truth. Bias is identifiable.

## Tier B — reputable analysis (cite freely; note the analyst)

- Established trade publications with named bylines (e.g., Financial Times, Stat News, The Information)
- Industry analyst firms with disclosed methodology (Gartner, Forrester, Pitchbook, CB Insights)
- Academic preprints from credentialed authors
- Long-form blogs by recognized practitioners (e.g., Stratechery, Matt Levine, Hardenberg)

**Why**: editorial standards or named expertise create accountability.

## Tier C — useful navigation, weak as citation

- Wikipedia (use to *find* primary sources; don't cite as evidence)
- Reddit/HN/X threads (use to *spot* viewpoints; cite the underlying source they reference, not the thread)
- Aggregator news sites that just summarize other reporting

**Why**: signal-rich for discovery, but evidence quality depends on what they link to. Follow the link and cite the original.

## Tier D — avoid as citations

- SEO content farms with undated articles ("10 Best X in 2026" with no byline)
- AI-generated summary sites (often hallucinate)
- Unsigned listicles
- Press releases laundered through aggregator sites (cite the original PR instead)
- Stale articles (>3 years old) for topics where currency matters (markets, regulation, technology)

**Why**: high hallucination/error risk; no accountability; often outdated.

## Quick checks before citing

1. **Has it got a byline?** No author → Tier C or lower.
2. **Is it dated?** No date → Tier D for time-sensitive topics.
3. **Does it cite its own sources?** If a claim is made without backing, don't trust the chain.
4. **Conflict of interest?** A vendor article hyping its own category is a Tier B *opinion*, not a fact.
5. **Currency matters?** For tech, finance, regulation, and politics, prefer sources from the last 12 months.

## Balance rule for a Pramana briefing

A briefing that cites only Tier A is rare and ideal. A briefing dominated by Tier C/D is unreliable. A healthy Pramana briefing usually has:

- 1–2 Tier A sources for the core facts
- 2–3 Tier B sources for analysis and context
- 0–1 Tier C only when it points to something the higher tiers don't cover
- Zero Tier D
