# The 9-Section Framework

Per-section content guidance for a Pramana briefing. Each section has a target shape, a common failure mode, and a quick test for "is this section done."

---

## 1. TL;DR

**Shape**: 3–5 declarative sentences. No bullets. No questions.

**Content**: What is this topic, why does it matter now, and what's the headline takeaway?

**Failure mode**: Hedging in the first sentence. "X is a complex and evolving area with many perspectives..." — useless. Open with the *claim*.

**Done test**: A reader who reads only the TL;DR walks away knowing what the briefing argues and why they should care.

---

## 2. Context

**Shape**: 1–2 paragraphs.

**Content**: Why this topic is on the agenda *right now*. What recent event, deadline, market shift, or trigger brings it into focus. Historical background only as much as is needed to make the present moment legible.

**Failure mode**: Treating Context as a Wikipedia-style backgrounder ("X was founded in 1998..."). Context is about *now*, not origin stories.

**Done test**: A reader understands why this briefing exists *today* rather than five years ago or five years from now.

---

## 3. Key facts

**Shape**: 4–8 bullet points. Each bullet is a single verifiable claim with a citation.

**Content**: The concrete data the rest of the briefing rests on. Numbers, dates, deal sizes, market shares, regulatory deadlines, technical specifications.

**Failure mode**: Bullets that are opinions or interpretations rather than facts. "X is the leader in the space" is a claim, not a fact. "X holds 34% market share per Gartner Q1 2026 [Source 3]" is a fact.

**Done test**: Each bullet can be independently verified by clicking its source.

---

## 4. Players / landscape

**Shape**: 2–4 short paragraphs OR a table, depending on what's clearer.

**Content**: Who is involved. For each major player: their position, what they want, what they bring, what constraints they're operating under.

**Failure mode**: Listing companies without their *position* or *incentive*. "Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Meta" — useless. "Anthropic is positioned as the safety-focused premium provider; pricing reflects this and they're constrained by compute supply." — useful.

**Done test**: A reader can predict each player's likely next move from what's written here.

---

## 5. Dynamics

**Shape**: 1–2 paragraphs.

**Content**: The forces in motion. What's accelerating, what's decelerating, what tensions exist between players or constraints. Cause → effect chains.

**Failure mode**: Restating the Players section as verbs. Dynamics is about the *interactions* — the field of forces, not the actors themselves.

**Done test**: A reader can articulate one specific cause-and-effect chain after reading this section.

---

## 6. Contrarian view

**Shape**: 1–2 paragraphs.

**Content**: The strongest case *against* the dominant narrative you've laid out so far. Steelman it. Cite at least one source that holds this view.

**Failure mode**: Strawmanning ("some critics worry that..."). Or preemptive rebuttal ("though critics claim X, the data shows Y"). Don't rebut. Hold the contrarian view as a live possibility.

**Done test**: A reader who agrees with the contrarian view feels they've been represented fairly.

---

## 7. Outlook

**Shape**: 1–2 paragraphs OR 2–3 scenarios.

**Content**: Where this is likely to go over the next 6–24 months. If there are clearly distinct scenarios, lay them out (base case / bull case / bear case). Otherwise a single best-estimate trajectory.

**Failure mode**: "Anything could happen" framing. If anything could happen, you haven't done the analysis. Even uncertain forecasts have a *central tendency*.

**Done test**: A reader has a specific event or threshold to watch for that will tell them whether the forecast is on track.

---

## 8. Recommendation / so-what

**Shape**: One paragraph. One call.

**Content**: What should the reader do, decide, watch, or stop doing. Name the tradeoff being accepted.

**Failure mode**: Three options dressed up as one recommendation. Or no concrete action at all ("stakeholders should consider these dynamics carefully"). If the briefing doesn't earn a clear recommendation, say so — but don't waffle.

**Done test**: A reader knows exactly one action to take or one belief to hold differently after reading this.

---

## 9. Sources

**Shape**: Numbered list. Each entry: `[Source N] Title — Publication — Date — URL`.

**Content**: Every source actually cited in the body. No padding with sources you didn't use.

**Failure mode**: Citing Wikipedia, AI-generated summaries, or undated content-mill articles. See `source-quality.md`.

**Done test**: Every `[Source N]` reference in the body matches a numbered entry here; every entry here is referenced at least once in the body.
